Defining the Decade: 1980–1989

While synthpop began as an experimental movement in the UK and Germany, by the mid-80s, it had completely taken over the Billboard charts. The combination of catchy pop melodies and futuristic electronic textures created some of the best-selling albums in music history.

The Chart-Toppers

These albums didn't just sell millions of copies; they defined the "sound" of the 1980s.

  1. Dare – The Human League (1981): Often cited as the album that proved synths could be "pop." It featured the massive hit "Don't You Want Me."
  2. Sweet Dreams (Are Made of This) – Eurythmics (1983): Annie Lennox and Dave Stewart created a dark, soulful version of electronic music that conquered the US charts.
  3. Some Great Reward – Depeche Mode (1984): This album pushed the genre into industrial territory, using sampled sounds of metal and machinery.
  4. Hunting High and Low – a-ha (1985): Known for "Take On Me," this album showcased how synthesizers could sound cinematic and grand.
  5. Technique – New Order (1989): As the decade closed, New Order blended synthpop with the emerging "Acid House" scene, pointing toward the future of rave music.

The Genre's Reach

Synthpop wasn't limited to just bands. It influenced solo artists like Michael Jackson (notably on Thriller) and Prince, who famously used the TR-808 and LinnDrum for many of his biggest hits.
